National Drought Summary for May 19, 2026

Summary

During the week, large parts of peninsular and central India continued to show rainfall deficits as the pre-monsoon period advanced, while the eastern Gangetic plains saw modest improvement. The Combined Drought Index pointed to severe-to-extreme conditions over interior Maharashtra, northern Karnataka, Telangana and parts of Rajasthan, driven by a combination of low standardised precipitation, declining soil moisture and reduced runoff. Reservoir storage across several western and southern basins remained below the ten-year average, sustaining short-term agricultural stress even where surface temperatures eased.

Northwest India

Persistent rainfall deficits kept much of western Rajasthan and parts of Gujarat in severe (D2) to extreme (D3) drought, with the standardised precipitation and soil-moisture indices both well below normal. Pockets of Haryana and southern Punjab showed abnormal dryness (D0) to moderate (D1) conditions. Irrigated tracts fared better than rain-fed areas, where the drying of the upper soil layer raised crop-stress signals in the index.

Central India

Madhya Pradesh and interior Maharashtra remained among the most affected regions. Extreme (D3) drought persisted across parts of Vidarbha and Marathwada, while large areas of Madhya Pradesh sat in the moderate (D1) to severe (D2) range. Low runoff and depleted soil moisture were the dominant contributors to the Combined Drought Index here, and reservoir levels in several Godavari and Tapi sub-basins stayed below their seasonal normals.

Peninsular & South India

Northern Karnataka, Telangana and Rayalaseema in Andhra Pradesh showed severe (D2) to extreme (D3) conditions, with rainfall well short of normal for the period. Coastal and southern Tamil Nadu were comparatively better, holding near-normal to abnormally dry (D0) conditions. Kerala and the Western Ghats remained largely outside drought designations, reflecting their high baseline moisture.

East & Northeast India

Improved soil moisture eased conditions across the eastern Gangetic plains, with parts of Bihar, Jharkhand and West Bengal stepping back from abnormal dryness (D0). The northeastern states generally remained free of drought designations, supported by their characteristically high rainfall and moisture, though isolated pockets showed short-term dryness.

Hilly & Northern Regions

The Himalayan states — Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand and Jammu & Kashmir — showed mostly near-normal conditions for the season, with the index reflecting adequate moisture at higher elevations. Localised dryness appeared in a few lower-elevation valleys but did not rise to widespread drought.

Island Territories

The Andaman & Nicobar Islands and Lakshadweep remained outside drought designations, consistent with their high baseline rainfall. Index values were near normal, with no clear moisture deficits observed over the week.

Looking Ahead

The short-range outlook for the coming weeks (reflected in the 7-, 15- and 30-day forecast indices on the monitor) suggests that drought conditions across central and peninsular India are likely to persist until the onset and progression of the southwest monsoon brings widespread rainfall. Until then, rain-fed regions in Maharashtra, Karnataka, Telangana and Rajasthan are expected to remain under the greatest short-term stress, while irrigated and higher-moisture regions hold steadier. Conditions will be reassessed as new data arrive.

Dryness Categories

The India Drought Monitor uses a five-category system to identify the unusualness of dry conditions. The categories range from D0 (Abnormally Dry) through D4 (Exceptional Drought). Each weekly map represents an integrated assessment combining objective indicators and on-the-ground observations from a network of local experts.

D0 Abnormally Dry
D1 Moderate Drought
D2 Severe Drought
D3 Extreme Drought
D4 Exceptional Drought

The India Drought Monitor is developed by the Water and Climate Lab at the Indian Institute of Technology Gandhinagar, drawing on hydro-meteorological data from the India Meteorological Department and the Central Water Commission, satellite products from ISRO/NRSC, and global datasets from NASA.
